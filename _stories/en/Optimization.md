Chapter I: Local Optimum
I am a lab administrator at EternalOpt BioTech. In this day and age, there are barely any real experiments left for humans to conduct.

Over two centuries ago, the advent of the Genomic Foundation Model fundamentally altered the trajectory of life sciences. Shortly thereafter, Gu Zhi built upon it to propose the "Multi-Objective Optimization Dynamics" algorithm—shortened to MOOD—for optimizing genomic pairing.

This algorithm could take any custom objective function—whether height, intelligence, athletic prowess, or abstract cognitive and reasoning faculties—and scour a massive donor registry to pinpoint the theoretically optimal zygotic combination. Gu Zhi proved that the outcome depended not on finding "exceptional individuals," but on achieving the "most suitable combination." Crucially, while MOOD enhanced individual phenotypes, it did not cause the human gene pool to converge or degenerate. On the back of this breakthrough, he founded EternalOpt BioTech.

As the first generation of MOOD infants grew into adulthood, every quantifiable metric—average height, health status, IQ—turned out to be flawlessly superior to any historical baseline. The sheer data silenced nearly all opposition. Eventually, the pairing of robotic companions and MOOD-generated offspring became the societal mainstream. For the individual, this mitigated risks on every front and rendered the quality of one's progeny absolute and controllable; for the collective, it resolved the sheer inefficiency of natural population evolution. On every conceivable scale, humanity was winning.

My job at EternalOpt was profoundly leisurely. My daily routine consisted of patrolling the incubation bays, checking the automated experimental platforms, and signing off on reports submitted by bots. A day’s work rarely took more than two hours. The rest of our time was spent lounging in the recreation zone, chatting and playing video games.

Until that afternoon, when the terminal flashed a deep, ominous crimson—a color it had never shown before.

Chapter II: Gradient Descent
The screen presented a grotesque contrast.

Severe traffic accidents, triggered by drivers suddenly losing consciousness, were erupting across multiple regions simultaneously. Within the very same office, one person would be desperately clawing at their own throat, their face turning from a sickly pale to bruised purple before dying of systemic vascular collapse within minutes; yet the colleague in the adjacent cubicle would look on, entirely unharmed, paralyzed by sheer shock.

An infuriated populace instantly branded EternalOpt as the architect of this catastrophe. Bricks and Molotov cocktails shattered the reinforced glass of the headquarters, while the peacekeeping droids deactivating en masse under waves of concentrated electromagnetic pulses.

Amidst the chaos of the siege, I stumbled back and accidentally slipped through a pneumatic door hidden behind a wall panel in the core laboratory. The door hissed shut and sealed tight behind me, completely sequestering the violence outside.

An outbreak of this magnitude could only mean the virus possessed an extraordinarily stealthy transmission capability. But why was it targeting almost exclusively those born through MOOD?

I pulled up the casualty data for the R&D and logistics departments. It wasn't correlated with occupation. It wasn't age. It was generational lineage. The earlier a family had adopted MOOD—and the higher their generational count—the higher their mortality rate. First-generation MOOD humans exhibited nothing more than a mild fever, whereas those of the fourth and fifth generations suffered near-absolute lethality.

This was a meticulously tuned harvest.

I groped my way through the pitch-black corridor. At the far end lay an utterly unremarkable office. A wooden desk, bookshelves, paper books—it looked like a room preserved from two centuries ago. The sole exception was a vintage terminal on the desk, still glowing with a faint, phosphor-blue light.

I walked over and entered my verification credentials. The screen displayed a global demographic distribution mapped against blinking red clusters of the epidemic. The data was clear: the virus used humans with low MOOD generations as a springboard for silent transmission; they were natural, asymptomatic hosts.

I attempted to calculate the precise demographic ratio required for the virus to achieve its maximum global transmission velocity. When the final curve stopped oscillating and flattened, I realized with horror that the perfect mathematical solution aligned flawlessly with the actual population structure of the current world.

"Your deduction is correct," a voice echoed from the shadows. "All of this was, indeed, designed."

Chapter III: Convergence
I spun around.

Gu Zhi stood by the bookshelf, looking almost identical to his historical holograms, save for a few more silver strands in his hair. He was Gu Zhi. As the progenitor of EternalOpt BioTech, it was hardly incomprehensible that he had possessed the means to keep himself alive until now.

"Why?" I asked, my voice trembling.

Gu Zhi stared at the tea leaves drifting in his cup, maintaining a brief silence. Sighing, he walked to the window and looked down at the city burning below.

"In my era," he began, "the technological explosion should have broken the curse of resource scarcity, guiding humanity toward true freedom. Instead, exponential gains in efficiency yielded nothing but a deeper, meaningless rat race. Advanced tech didn't eliminate anxiety; it merely magnified everyone's obsession with 'optimizing' everything: a better degree, a better job, more money, a more flawless spouse... Why should a human civilization this advanced still wage a savage struggle for survival like primitive beasts?"

"Because while our civilization boarded a rocket, the human genome remained stranded in the ancient, primeval wild," Gu Zhi’s voice carried no warmth whatsoever. He explained, "In that harsh, resource-depleted antiquity, 'greed' was a vital weapon to secure individual survival and genetic continuity. But when humanity entered the modern era, greed morphed into a systemic cancer. Yet, in an environment where the majority defaults to greed, it becomes a strictly dominant strategy. Those who don't fight, don't grab, and remain content are ruthlessly culled. I couldn't purge human desire, but I could use MOOD to detonate their greed. I offered them an un-refusable optimization—an optimization at the fundamental biological level—as a gift."

He tapped the desktop, summoning a complex holographic molecular structure.

"For two centuries, to ensure that MOOD zygotes exhibited early developmental speeds and stress resistance far beyond the natural norm, EternalOpt infused an exceptionally potent mitogenic factor into all cultivation media. The agent was flawless for embryonic acceleration, but it came at a cost: it severely aggravated replication stress, drastically increasing the probability of replication slippage."

Gu Zhi ran a finger along the holographic molecular chain and continued:

"Initially, this slippage triggered no conventional diseases; it merely introduced repetitive sequences that seemed to enhance genetic diversity. However, as the generational lineage of MOOD offspring stacked over time, these sequence repetitions accumulated exponentially. Consequently, on the surfaces of these new humans' immune cells and vascular endothelial cells, the hydrophobic residue tails of a certain adhesion receptor were progressively elongated. What were once scattered surface receptors began to cluster on the cell membranes, their density compounding geometrically with each successive generation."

"Two hundred years ago, I named that algorithm MOOD," Gu Zhi said, staring at the interwoven genetic strands, his tone remaining ice-cold. "And the virus unleashed today, I call DOOM."

"As a meticulously engineered masterwork, its first site binds exclusively to cells in non-core tissues, allowing it to propagate through the populace like a mundane flu. Its second site, however, locks onto those surface hydrophobic receptor clusters with absolute specificity. The larger the cluster, the higher the binding success rate, and the faster the vascular and immune systems collapse."

"The repetitive sequences accumulated by MOOD are the receipts of greed stacked by humanity for two centuries. And I am merely executing the sentence."

Chapter IV: Halting
The disaster gradually subsided after raging for several weeks. The global population had plummeted by nearly two-thirds, with those of the highest generational count and deepest optimization almost entirely wiped out. The cataclysm left the survivors with profound trauma and a lingering shadow; empty, dead-silent streets juxtaposed against grand, pristine architecture, wrapping the remnants of civilization in a surreal sense of desolation.

Yet, the surviving humans displayed a staggering resilience in the wake of this monumental upheaval. Leveraging the highly mature automated production lines, intelligent scheduling networks, and infrastructural systems, they began repairing the fractured social apparatus, rediscovering a sense of order through the daily grind of reconstruction.

Amidst the shared burdens of day-to-day survival, people experienced a long-forgotten tranquility for the very first time. No one knew where this deeply scarred world would ultimately rest, nor toward what horizon the future of human civilization would sail.
